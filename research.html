Research

Research agenda

My research agenda investigates why states pursue, abandon, or refrain from nuclear weapons, and how state and non-state actors behave in cyberspace under conditions of competition short of war. I combine quantitative methods, including survival models and machine learning, with historical case studies to study nuclear decision-making. I also use survey experiments and tabletop exercises to study cyber conflict, with an emerging focus on how AI shapes both proliferation dynamics and public perceptions of cybersecurity.

Dissertation

Analyzing the Causes of Nuclear Proliferation: A Multi-Method Approach

Expected defense — April 2027

This dissertation examines the causes of nuclear proliferation using a mixed-methods approach that combines quantitative tools, machine learning, and historical case studies. The findings indicate that security concerns remain a central driver of proliferation decisions, while technological factors — particularly the transfer of dual-use technologies — have grown increasingly important in recent decades.

  1. Updating the determinants of nuclear proliferation — replicates Jo and Gartzke (2007) with an expanded dataset covering 1939–2014.
  2. Updating the correlates of nuclear proliferation — replicates Singh and Way (2004) with an expanded dataset covering 1939–2014.
  3. A machine learning approach to nuclear proliferation — applies the expanded Jo and Gartzke dataset using three machine learning methods: Multivariate Adaptive Regression Splines, Random Forests, and Neural Networks.
  4. Status, Prestige, and the Bomb — argues that status and prestige are important drivers of nuclear pursuit, useful for explaining cases not accounted for by rationalist causes, via three pathways (rising powers, declining powers, and cause attachment) illustrated through case studies of the Shah's Iran, Gaddafi's Libya, and Argentina.

Working papers

Nuclear Stories of Taiwan and Israel: A Case Study into the Nuclear Window Approach

Under submission at Security Studies

Why have so few states acquired nuclear weapons, despite widespread security motivations and growing technological capabilities? This paper introduces the concept of a "nuclear window of opportunity," arguing that successful proliferation requires the alignment of four conditions: motivating demand-side and enabling supply-side factors, and the absence of discouraging demand-side and constraining supply-side factors. Partial alignment may result in nuclear exploration or pursuit, but acquisition requires all four factors to align. I apply this concept to compare the nuclear trajectories of Israel and Taiwan — two states with similar backgrounds but different proliferation outcomes — showing that Israel experienced a sustained alignment of all four factors enabling acquisition, while Taiwan never did. By integrating existing explanations into a conditional, multi-causal framework, the nuclear window approach explains both the rarity of successful proliferation and variation in proliferation outcomes.
