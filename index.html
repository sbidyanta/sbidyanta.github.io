I am a PhD candidate in Political Science at the School of Public and International Affairs, University of Cincinnati, with an expected defense date of April 2027. My research examines the causes of nuclear proliferation through a mixed-methods approach combining quantitative analysis, machine learning, and historical case studies, alongside work on cyber conflict, deterrence, and public attitudes toward escalation in cyberspace. I am on the market for assistant professor positions in nuclear security, cybersecurity policy, and international relations. I can teach classes in International Security, International Organizations, as well as a full sequence of quantitative methods (including survey methods).

Recent Writing

Anthropic's Fable Debacle and the Perils of Dual-use AI Technologies

Geopolitical Monitor

In this article, I argue that the launch and subsequent shutting down of Claude Fable is only the tip of the iceberg of dual-use AI. While regulating AI is more necessary than ever, the inherent challenges in regulating something that only exists in the virtual domain, combined with the industry's opposition to government regulation, means that substantial policy challenges remain.

Much Ado About Nothing: The Proliferation Debate Post Venezuela

Global Security Review

In this article, I look at whether the US intervention in Venezuela meaningfully increases the global incentives for nuclear proliferation, and argue that the systemic effects will be rather limited. I also argue that despite the turbulent nature of international politics recently, the odds of another wave of proliferation is very low.
